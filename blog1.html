BREAST CANCER

Sept 23rd 2025 3 min read

Think of your body as a well-organized city—every cell knows its job and follows the rules. But sometimes, a few rebel cells decide they no longer want to obey. They grow faster, refuse to die, and begin to take over their neighborhood. That’s how breast cancer begins—when normal breast cells turn rogue and start multiplying uncontrollably.

Breast cancer is one of the most common cancers in the world, affecting millions of people each year. Though it mostly occurs in women, men can also get breast cancer because everyone is born with some amount of breast tissue.

How It Starts

Inside each cell lies a DNA “instruction manual.” In healthy cells, this manual tells them when to grow, divide, and rest. But when certain mutations—changes in DNA—occur, the instructions get scrambled. These abnormal cells form a lump, known as a tumor, which can invade surrounding tissues or even spread to other parts of the body.

Spotting the Signs Early

Like any enemy, breast cancer gives warning signs before it strikes hard. Common symptoms include:

  • A lump or swelling in the breast or underarm
  • Changes in breast size or shape
  • Redness, flaking, or dimpling of the breast skin
  • Nipple discharge or pain

Early detection can make all the difference—regular self-examinations and screenings (mammograms) can help catch it before it spreads.

Breast cancer is not a death sentence—it’s a challenge that can be fought and won. Awareness, early detection, and regular checkups remain the strongest weapons we have.

Who Is at Risk?

Some risk factors are beyond our control—like age, genetics (BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations), and family history. Others, such as obesity, alcohol use, smoking, and lack of physical activity, can be managed through lifestyle choices. Hormonal changes and exposure to radiation can also increase risk.

Hope in the Fight

The good news? Survival rates have greatly improved thanks to early detection, better treatments, and awareness campaigns. Treatments include surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy, and targeted or hormone therapy—each designed to destroy cancer cells or stop them from spreading.
