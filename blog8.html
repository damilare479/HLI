BREAST CANCER IN MEN

30 Sept 2025 5 min read

The Unexpected Truth

Meet Mr. Adewunmi, a 65 years old retired university lecturer who had always prided himself on staying fit. One morning, while getting ready, he noticed a small, firm lump just beneath his left nipple. “Strange,” he thought. “Maybe it’s just a muscle knot.” Weeks passed, but the lump didn’t go away it grew. When he finally went to the hospital, the doctor’s words stunned him: “You have breast cancer”. He exclaimed: “Ahhhh! As how? Isn’t this meant to be a woman thing?!”

Like most people, Mr. Adewunmi didn’t even know that men could get breast cancer. After all, isn’t that supposed to be a “woman’s disease”? But here’s the twist: about one in every 1,000 men is at risk. According to the National Breast Cancer Foundation, “All people, whether male or female, are born with some breast cells and tissue. Even though males do not develop milk-producing breasts, a man’s breast cells and tissue can still develop cancer.”

Spotting the Warning Signs

Mr. Adewunmi’s experience isn’t unique he just noticed what many ignore. The symptoms in men mirror those in women: a lump in the breast or underarm, a change in size or shape, redness of the breast skin, or nipple discharge. Sometimes, the signs are subtle, but silence can be deadly.

When Cells Go Rogue

Think of the body as a city, and your DNA as the rulebook that keeps every cell in line. In healthy cells, DNA says, “Grow when needed, stop when it’s time.” But in cancer cells, that rulebook gets rewritten. The new orders? “Grow fast. Never die.”

When these rebellious cells pile up, they form a tumor a growing “gang” that invades neighboring tissue and can spread to distant organs. This is how breast cancer begins its takeover, silently and steadily.

Why Men Aren’t Exempt

Everyone, men and women are born with a small amount of breast tissue made up of milk ducts, glands, and fat. During puberty, women develop more, while men don’t. But that little bit left behind is enough for cancer to take root. The myth that “men can’t get breast cancer” isn’t just wrong, it’s dangerous

Types of Male Breast Cancer

Every story has its villains, and breast cancer comes in different forms:

  • Ductal Carcinoma: The most common, starting in the milk ducts near the nipple.
  • Lobular Carcinoma: Begins in milk-producing glands (rare in men since they have fewer lobules)
  • Other Rare Forms: Like Paget’s disease of the nipple or inflammatory breast cancer uncommon but aggressive.

Unmasking the Risk Factors

Behind every diagnosis lies a set of triggers; the quiet conspirators.

  • Age: The older you get, the higher the risk.
  • Hormone Therapy: Using estrogen-based drugs (for example, in prostate treatment) increases vulnerability
  • Family History and Genes: Having a close relative with breast cancer, or inheriting mutations like BRCA1 or BRCA2, raises your chances.
  • ⁠Klinefelter Syndrome: A rare genetic disorder involving an extra X chromosome that disrupts hormone balance
  • Liver Disease and Obesity: Both conditions elevate estrogen levels in the body.

These aren’t just medical facts; they’re the hidden pathways that led to Mr Adewunmi’s diagnosis.

Male breast cancer may be rare, but it’s real and it’s no respecter of gender. Awareness is the first line of defense. Check yourself. Know your risks. Listen to your body.

Can It Be Prevented?

There’s no guaranteed way to stop male breast cancer, but knowledge and vigilance can change everything.

If breast cancer runs in your family, consider genetic testing to check for risky mutations like BRCA1 or BRCA2. Regular checkups and self-examination can help spot early signs before they turn deadly

Mr. Adewunmi’s had I know moment

After weeks of treatment, Mr. Adewunmi became an advocate for awareness of breast cancer in male. “Had I know, I won’t have ignored it longer, I might not be here,” he says with a quiet sad face. His story reminds us that early detection saves lives and that silence can be the most dangerous symptom of all.

Like Mr. Adewunmi discovered, it’s not the enemy you see coming that defeats you, it’s the one you never thought existed
